## SECTION 2: PRE-CLASSICAL MATHEMATICS (~20,000 BCE - 500 BCE) -- Slides 4-14

### Slide 4: Section Divider -- "Where It All Began"
- **Visual:** Map showing Africa, the Fertile Crescent, Nile Valley, and Mesoamerica
- **Timeline marker:** 20,000 BCE - 500 BCE highlighted

### Slide 5: The Ishango Bone -- The Oldest Mathematics?
- **Key content:** The Ishango bone (c. 20,000 BCE, discovered 1960 near Lake Edward, modern Democratic Republic of the Congo) -- a bone tool with notched markings that may represent arithmetic, prime numbers, or a lunar calendar. One of the earliest known mathematical artifacts.
- **Visual:** Photograph of the Ishango bone (Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences, Brussels); close-up of the tally marks
- **Source:** Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences (digitized photos available)
- **Talking point:** "Mathematics may be as old as humanity itself. 20,000 years before Babylon, someone in Central Africa was scratching mathematical patterns into bone."
- **[VERIFY]:** The interpretation of the Ishango bone is debated. Some scholars (Marshack, De Heinzelin) see mathematical significance; others see it as a simple tally or tool handle. Present as "possibly" mathematical.

### Slide 6: Babylon -- The First Mathematicians
- **Key content:** Babylonian base-60 number system (why we have 60 minutes in an hour, 360 degrees in a circle)
- **Visual:** Photograph of a cuneiform clay tablet with mathematical content
- **Source:** Yale Babylonian Collection or British Museum digitized tablets
- **Talking point:** "The next time you check the clock, thank a Babylonian scribe from 4,000 years ago"

### Slide 7: Plimpton 322 -- A 3,800-Year-Old Mystery
- **Person focus:** Anonymous Babylonian scribe(s), c. 1800 BCE, Mesopotamia (modern Iraq)
- **Key content:** Plimpton 322 tablet -- contains Pythagorean triples, roughly 1,200 years BEFORE Pythagoras
- **Visual:** High-resolution photo of Plimpton 322 (Columbia University collection)
- **Source:** Columbia University Rare Book & Manuscript Library (public domain photos available)
- **Note on attribution:** "The theorem we call 'Pythagorean' was known to Babylonians over a millennium earlier. Math rarely has a single inventor."
- **[VERIFY]:** The exact interpretation of Plimpton 322 is debated. Neugebauer & Sachs (1945) interpreted it as Pythagorean triples; Robson (2001) proposed it as a teacher's aid. Both interpretations should be mentioned.

### Slide 8: Egyptian Mathematics -- Practical Genius
- **Key content:** Egyptian mathematics was driven by practical needs: land surveying after Nile floods, architecture, taxation
- **Visual:** Section of the Rhind Papyrus (British Museum, EA 10057-8)
- **Source:** British Museum digitized collection
- **Transition note:** The Rhind Papyrus and the Moscow Papyrus are our two main sources for Egyptian mathematics. The Rhind Papyrus (next slide) is the larger and more complete; the Moscow Papyrus (Slide 10) contains different problems, including the remarkable frustum formula.

### Slide 9: Ahmes (also: Ahmose)
- **Full name:** Ahmes (also written Ahmose)
- **Dates:** fl. c. 1550 BCE [VERIFY]
- **Origin:** Ancient Egypt
- **Contribution:** Scribe who copied (and possibly compiled) the Rhind Mathematical Papyrus -- our best source on Egyptian mathematics. Contains problems in arithmetic, algebra, geometry, including a formula for the area of a circle that implies pi ~ 3.16
- **Visual:** Portion of Rhind Papyrus showing a geometry problem; reconstruction of what Ahmes's working environment may have looked like
- **Source:** British Museum digital collection
- **Note:** Ahmes was a scribe, not necessarily the inventor of the methods. He wrote: "Accurate reckoning for inquiring into things, and the knowledge of all things, mysteries... all secrets." [VERIFY exact wording of this translation]

### Slide 10: Egyptian Geometry in Action [EXTENDED]
- **Key content:** The Moscow Papyrus (c. 1850 BCE, a separate document from the Rhind Papyrus) contains a formula for the volume of a truncated pyramid (frustum) that is exactly correct. The Rhind Papyrus gives a method for calculating circle area (octagon approximation).
- **Visual:** Diagram showing the Egyptian method for circle area (octagon approximation) alongside the modern formula; the Moscow Papyrus frustum formula
- **Talking point:** "They got pi accurate to within 1% -- without calculus, without computers, using reeds and papyrus"

### Slide 11: Mesopotamian Algebra [EXTENDED]
- **Key content:** Babylonians could solve quadratic equations (in verbal/geometric form, not symbolic algebra)
- **Visual:** Translation of a Babylonian word problem alongside its modern algebraic equivalent
- **Talking point:** "Your algebra homework has a 4,000-year pedigree"
- **Interactive moment:** Think-Pair-Share -- "Why do you think the Babylonians never developed the concept of mathematical proof? Was their approach inferior, or just different?" (2-3 min discussion)

### Slide 12: What Was Missing?
- **Key content:** Ancient math was computational, not axiomatic. No concept of "proof" as the Greeks would develop it. This is not a deficiency -- it served its purpose brilliantly.
- **Visual:** Comparison table: Babylonian approach (recipe/procedure) vs. Greek approach (theorem/proof)

### Slide 13: Mayan Mathematics -- An Independent Tradition
- **Key content:** The Maya civilization (c. 4th century CE and earlier, Mesoamerica) independently developed a vigesimal (base-20) number system and, remarkably, invented the concept of zero independently of Indian mathematicians. Their Long Count calendar required sophisticated positional arithmetic.
- **Visual:** Mayan numeral system (dots and bars); a Mayan zero glyph; section of a Mayan calendar stone
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons
- **Talking point:** "Zero was invented independently at least twice in human history -- in India and in Mesoamerica. The Maya used it centuries before Europe even knew the concept existed."
- **[VERIFY]:** The exact dating of the earliest Mayan zero is debated. A stele from Tres Zapotes (Epi-Olmec, 36 BCE) may contain an early Long Count date, but the classic Mayan zero glyph appears firmly by the 4th century CE. Clarify which dating is used.

### Slide 14: Legacy of Ancient Mathematics
- **Key content:** Base-60 system, basic algebra, geometry for architecture and surveying, early approximations of pi, independent number systems across continents, the concept of zero appearing independently in multiple civilizations
- **Visual:** Infographic showing ancient math concepts that survive today (clock, compass, calendar); small world map showing independent mathematical traditions
- **Transition:** "But one civilization would transform math from a tool into a way of thinking..."

## SECTION 3: GREEK MATHEMATICS (~600 BCE - 300 CE) -- Slides 15-24

### Slide 15: Section Divider -- "The Greek Revolution: Math Becomes Logic"
- **Visual:** Map of ancient Greece and the Hellenistic world (including Alexandria)
- **Timeline marker:** 600 BCE - 300 CE

### Slide 16: Thales of Miletus
- **Full name:** Thales of Miletus (Greek: Thales ho Milesios)
- **Dates:** c. 624 - c. 546 BCE [traditional dates; exact dates uncertain]
- **Origin:** Miletus, Ionia (modern Turkey)
- **Contribution:** Traditionally regarded as the first person to use deductive reasoning in mathematics. Credited with Thales' theorem (any angle inscribed in a semicircle is a right angle). Also credited with predicting a solar eclipse. [VERIFY: the eclipse prediction story may be legendary]
- **Visual:** Diagram of Thales' theorem; map showing Miletus
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons

### Slide 17: Pythagoras and the Pythagoreans
- **Full name:** Pythagoras of Samos (Greek: Pythagoras ho Samios)
- **Dates:** c. 570 - c. 495 BCE
- **Origin:** Samos, Greece; later active in Croton, Magna Graecia (modern southern Italy)
- **Contribution:** Founded a school/community that saw numbers as the foundation of reality. The Pythagorean theorem (a^2 + b^2 = c^2) bears his name, though as noted, it was known earlier. Discovery that the square root of 2 is irrational -- a crisis for their philosophy. [VERIFY: whether Pythagoras himself or his followers made these discoveries is debated; ancient sources attribute much to "the Pythagoreans" collectively]
- **Visual:** Classic Pythagorean theorem diagram with area squares; possibly a Renaissance-era depiction of Pythagoras
- **Talking point:** "When they discovered irrational numbers, it reportedly caused a philosophical crisis -- legend says they tried to keep it secret"

### Slide 18: Euclid -- The Elements
- **Full name:** Euclid of Alexandria (Greek: Eukleides)
- **Dates:** fl. c. 300 BCE (birth and death dates unknown)
- **Origin:** Likely active in Alexandria, Ptolemaic Egypt
- **Contribution:** Wrote "The Elements" -- the most influential mathematics textbook in history, used for over 2,000 years. Organized geometry into a deductive system from axioms. Proved the infinitude of prime numbers.
- **Visual:** Page from a medieval manuscript of the Elements (many available from Bodleian Library, Vatican Library); diagram of Euclid's proof of the Pythagorean theorem (Book I, Proposition 47)
- **Source:** Bodleian Library digitized manuscripts; Wikimedia Commons

### Slide 19: Archimedes -- The Greatest?
- **Full name:** Archimedes of Syracuse (Greek: Archimedes ho Syrakoúsios)
- **Dates:** c. 287 - 212 BCE
- **Origin:** Syracuse, Sicily (then a Greek colony)
- **Contribution:** Calculated pi to high accuracy (between 3 10/71 and 3 1/7) using inscribed/circumscribed polygons. Discovered principles of buoyancy and the lever. Used the method of exhaustion to compute areas and volumes of curved surfaces, in a way that some historians compare to early integral calculus. [VERIFY: the characterization of Archimedes's method of exhaustion as "anticipating integral calculus" is debated among historians. Some see it as a genuine precursor; others argue it is fundamentally different in concept. Present as a comparison, not an identity.]
- **Visual:** The Archimedes Palimpsest (Walters Art Museum); diagram of his polygon method for pi
- **Source:** Walters Art Museum digital collection (Archimedes Palimpsest is public domain)
- **Talking point:** "Archimedes was killed by a Roman soldier during the siege of Syracuse, reportedly saying 'Do not disturb my circles'" [VERIFY: this is from Plutarch and may be legendary]

### Slide 20: Eratosthenes -- Measuring the Earth
- **Full name:** Eratosthenes of Cyrene (Greek: Eratosthenes ho Kyrenaios)
- **Dates:** c. 276 - c. 194 BCE
- **Origin:** Cyrene (modern Libya); worked in Alexandria
- **Contribution:** Calculated the Earth's circumference using shadow angles at Alexandria and Syene (modern Aswan) -- remarkably close to the true value. Also developed the Sieve of Eratosthenes for finding prime numbers.
- **Visual:** Diagram showing his shadow measurement method; the Sieve of Eratosthenes as an animation/step diagram
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons

### Slide 21: Apollonius -- Conic Sections [EXTENDED]
- **Full name:** Apollonius of Perga (Greek: Apollonios ho Pergaios)
- **Dates:** c. 262 - c. 190 BCE
- **Origin:** Perga, Pamphylia (modern Turkey); worked in Alexandria
- **Contribution:** Wrote "Conics" -- the definitive ancient work on conic sections (ellipse, parabola, hyperbola). These names are his. His work would be essential for Kepler's planetary orbits 1,800 years later.
- **Visual:** Diagram of a cone being sliced to produce the four conic sections
- **Talking point:** "Apollonius named the curves you study in precalculus. Kepler would later show that planets travel in the ellipses Apollonius described."

### Slide 22: Diophantus -- Father of Algebra?
- **Full name:** Diophantus of Alexandria (Greek: Diophantos ho Alexandreus)
- **Dates:** fl. c. 250 CE [VERIFY: dates very uncertain, estimates range from 200-284 CE]
- **Origin:** Alexandria, Roman Egypt
- **Contribution:** Wrote "Arithmetica" -- a collection of algebraic problems seeking integer or rational solutions. Introduced symbolic abbreviations for unknowns and powers -- one of the earliest uses of proto-algebraic notation (though not yet a fully systematic symbolic algebra as Viete would later develop). [VERIFY: qualify the claim that Diophantus "introduced" symbolic abbreviations -- earlier civilizations used some symbolic shorthand. His contribution was more systematic but not entirely unprecedented.] "Diophantine equations" (integer solutions) are named after him. Fermat's famous "Last Theorem" was scribbled in the margin of a copy of Arithmetica.
- **Visual:** Page from a manuscript of Arithmetica; Fermat's marginal note
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons

### Slide 23: Hypatia of Alexandria
- **Full name:** Hypatia of Alexandria (Greek: Hypatia he Alexandrine)
- **Dates:** c. 350-370 - 415 CE [VERIFY: birth year uncertain; 355 or 370 CE are common estimates]
- **Origin:** Alexandria, Roman Egypt
- **Contribution:** Mathematician, astronomer, and philosopher. Wrote commentaries on Diophantus's Arithmetica and Apollonius's Conics. [VERIFY: whether she edited Arithmetica (wrote new commentary) or merely revised an existing commentary is debated. Some sources credit her with substantive editorial work on Book III of Arithmetica; others are more cautious.] Head of the Neoplatonist school in Alexandria. Murdered by a Christian mob in 415 CE.
- **Visual:** The 1885 Charles William Mitchell painting "Hypatia"; or Raphael's "The School of Athens" detail [VERIFY: her presence in the painting is debated -- use Mitchell painting as primary]
- **Talking point:** "She was one of the last great scholars of ancient Alexandria, and her murder is sometimes seen as a symbol of the end of classical learning"

### Slide 24: Greek Legacy and Transition
- **Key content:** The Greeks gave us axiomatic reasoning, proof, and the idea that math is about eternal truths, not just practical calculation. But much Greek knowledge would be lost to Western Europe and preserved by... (transition to next sections)
- **Visual:** Map showing the flow of Greek texts: originals lost in the West, preserved in Byzantine and Islamic libraries
- **Transition:** "Greek mathematical texts were about to take two very different journeys -- east to India and the Islamic world, and into near-oblivion in medieval Europe"

## SECTION 4: INDIAN MATHEMATICS (~500 BCE - 1200 CE) -- Slides 25-31

### Slide 25: Section Divider -- "India: The Invention of Nothing (and Everything)"
- **Visual:** Stylized zero symbol with Sanskrit/Brahmi numerals
- **Timeline marker:** 500 BCE - 1200 CE

### Slide 26: Early Indian Mathematics
- **Key content:** Indian mathematics has deep roots in the Sulba Sutras (c. 800-200 BCE) -- geometric rules for altar construction that include Pythagorean triples and approximations of the square root of 2
- **Visual:** Diagram from the Sulba Sutras showing altar construction geometry
- **[VERIFY]:** Dating of Sulba Sutras varies by source. The Baudhayana Sulba Sutra is generally dated c. 800 BCE but some scholars place it later.

### Slide 27: Aryabhata
- **Full name:** Aryabhata (sometimes Aryabhata I, to distinguish from a later mathematician of the same name)
- **Dates:** 476 - 550 CE
- **Origin:** Kusumapura (likely modern Patna), Gupta Empire, India
- **Contribution:** Wrote "Aryabhatiya" (499 CE) -- gave pi as approximately 3.1416 (remarkably accurate), used a place-value number system, tabulated sine values, proposed that the Earth rotates on its axis. His work on indeterminate equations (kuttaka method) anticipated later results.
- **Visual:** Statue of Aryabhata (modern, at IUCAA Pune) [VERIFY location]; page from a manuscript of Aryabhatiya
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons

### Slide 28: Brahmagupta
- **Full name:** Brahmagupta
- **Dates:** 598 - c. 668 CE
- **Origin:** Bhinmal, Rajasthan, India
- **Contribution:** Wrote "Brahmasphutasiddhanta" (628 CE). First mathematician to formalize rules for arithmetic with zero and negative numbers (including 0 + 0 = 0, a negative times a negative is positive). Gave a general solution to the quadratic equation. Brahmagupta's formula for the area of a cyclic quadrilateral.
- **Visual:** The rules for zero written in modern notation alongside the original Sanskrit; diagram of a cyclic quadrilateral
- **Talking point:** "Brahmagupta wrote the rules for zero that you use every day -- thirteen centuries ago"
- **[VERIFY]:** Brahmagupta's treatment of 0/0 (he said 0/0 = 0) is mathematically incorrect but historically important. Mention this nuance.

### Slide 29: Bhaskara II (Bhaskaracharya)
- **Full name:** Bhaskara II (Bhaskaracharya, "Bhaskara the Teacher")
- **Dates:** 1114 - 1185 CE
- **Origin:** Bijjada Bida (likely in modern Karnataka), India
- **Contribution:** Wrote "Lilavati" (a math textbook addressed to his daughter) and "Bijaganita" (algebra). Advanced understanding of the number system, solved Pell's equation (x^2 - ny^2 = 1) before Pell. Preliminary concepts related to infinitesimals and what we now call derivatives.
- **Visual:** Opening verse of Lilavati in Devanagari with translation; portrait (if available, likely later artistic depiction)
- **Talking point:** "He addressed his math textbook to his daughter Lilavati -- making it one of the earliest math textbooks written with a specific student in mind"
- **[VERIFY]:** The story of Lilavati being his daughter and the astrological tale around her is traditional but may be legend.

### Slide 30: The Kerala School
- **Key content:** Madhava of Sangamagrama (c. 1340 - c. 1425 CE, Kerala, India) and his followers developed infinite series for pi, sine, and cosine BEFORE European discovery of the same. This includes the Madhava-Leibniz series for pi and the Madhava-Newton power series for sine and cosine.
- **Visual:** The Madhava-Leibniz series written out; map of Kerala; timeline comparing Kerala school results with later European rediscoveries
- **[VERIFY]:** The extent to which Kerala school results were transmitted to Europe (if at all) is debated. Most historians believe independent rediscovery, but some argue for possible transmission via Jesuit missionaries. Present this as an open question.
- **Persons:**
  - **Madhava of Sangamagrama** -- c. 1340 - c. 1425 CE, Kerala, India. Founder of the Kerala school. Infinite series for pi, sine, cosine.
  - **Nilakantha Somayaji** -- 1444 - 1544 CE, Kerala, India. Wrote "Tantrasamgraha" (1501). Extended Madhava's work. [VERIFY exact death year]

### Slide 31: Indian Mathematics Legacy
- **Key content:** India gave the world: the decimal place-value system, zero as a number, negative numbers, trigonometric functions (sine), infinite series. These ideas flowed to the Islamic world and then to Europe.
- **Visual:** Flow diagram: Indian numerals --> Arabic numerals --> "Hindu-Arabic" numerals used worldwide today
- **Transition:** "Indian mathematical ideas would soon be taken up, extended, and transformed by scholars writing in Arabic..."

## SECTION 5: CHINESE MATHEMATICS (~200 BCE - 1300 CE) -- Slides 32-36

### Slide 32: Section Divider -- "China: Parallel Discoveries"
- **Visual:** Page from "The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art"; Chinese rod numerals
- **Timeline marker:** 200 BCE - 1300 CE

### Slide 33: The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art
- **Key content:** "Jiuzhang Suanshu" (Nine Chapters) -- compiled c. 2nd century BCE to 1st century CE. A collection of 246 problems covering arithmetic, algebra, geometry, and engineering. Includes Gaussian elimination (roughly 2,000 years before Gauss), and methods for computing square and cube roots.
- **Visual:** Page from the Nine Chapters; diagram of the "broken bamboo" problem
- **Source:** Wikimedia Commons; Chinese library digital collections
- **[VERIFY]:** The dating of the Nine Chapters is debated. The text was compiled over centuries, with the final form usually dated to the 1st century CE (Han Dynasty).

### Slide 34: Liu Hui
- **Full name:** Liu Hui
- **Dates:** fl. c. 263 CE
- **Origin:** Cao Wei kingdom, China (Three Kingdoms period)
- **Contribution:** Wrote a commentary on the Nine Chapters (263 CE). Used a polygon method (similar to Archimedes) to calculate pi to 5 decimal places (pi ~ 3.14159). Developed principles of cartographic surveying.
- **Visual:** Liu Hui's polygon method diagram for pi; comparison with Archimedes' method
- **[VERIFY]:** The exact decimal accuracy Liu Hui achieved is sometimes stated as 3.1416 (4 decimal places) or 3.14159 (5 places). Sources vary.

### Slide 35: Zu Chongzhi
- **Full name:** Zu Chongzhi
- **Dates:** 429 - 500 CE
- **Origin:** Liu Song / Southern Qi dynasties, China
- **Contribution:** Calculated pi to 7 decimal places (between 3.1415926 and 3.1415927) -- the most accurate value in the world for nearly 1,000 years. Also approximated pi as 355/113, which is accurate to 6 decimal places.
- **Visual:** The fraction 355/113 highlighted; comparison timeline of pi accuracy across civilizations
- **Talking point:** "His approximation 355/113 is so good that it was not bettered for a millennium"

### Slide 36: Chinese Mathematics Legacy [EXTENDED]
- **Key content:** Chinese mathematicians independently developed many techniques (Gaussian elimination, Horner's method, Pascal's triangle [known as Yang Hui's triangle in China]). The Chinese Remainder Theorem (attributed to Sunzi, c. 3rd-5th century CE [VERIFY]) remains important in modern cryptography.
- **Persons additionally mentioned:**
  - **Sunzi (mathematician)** -- fl. c. 3rd-5th century CE [VERIFY], China. Stated the Chinese Remainder Theorem in "Sunzi Suanjing."
  - **Yang Hui** -- c. 1238 - 1298 CE, Song Dynasty China. Described "Yang Hui's triangle" (Pascal's triangle) in 1261. [VERIFY dates]
  - **Zhu Shijie** -- c. 1249 - 1314 CE, Yuan Dynasty China. Wrote "Jade Mirror of the Four Unknowns" (1303). Advanced polynomial algebra with up to four unknowns. [VERIFY dates]
- **Visual:** Yang Hui's triangle from the original Chinese text alongside Pascal's triangle
- **Transition:** "Now we arrive at one of the most transformative periods in mathematical history -- one that is too often overlooked in Western textbooks..."

## SECTION 6: ISLAMIC GOLDEN AGE MATHEMATICS (~800 - 1400 CE) -- Slides 37-50

**NOTE: This is intentionally the largest single section. The Islamic Golden Age is a CORE part of the mathematical story, not a sidebar.**

### Slide 37: Section Divider -- "The House of Wisdom: When Baghdad Was the Center of the World"
- **Visual:** Artistic reconstruction of the House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma) in Baghdad; map of the Islamic world c. 900 CE
- **Timeline marker:** 800-1400 CE highlighted
- **Design note:** This divider should be visually striking -- it marks a major thematic shift

### Slide 38: The Translation Movement
- **Key content:** Under the Abbasid Caliphate, a massive translation project preserved and translated Greek, Indian, and Persian texts into Arabic. Without this effort, much of Greek mathematics might have been permanently lost. The House of Wisdom in Baghdad was the epicenter.
- **Visual:** Flow diagram: Greek texts (some lost originals) --> Syriac/Arabic translations --> Latin translations --> Renaissance Europe
- **Talking point:** "When Europeans later 'rediscovered' Greek mathematics, they were often translating Arabic translations of Greek originals that no longer existed in Greek"
- **[VERIFY]:** The House of Wisdom's exact nature is debated. Some historians (e.g., Gutas) describe it primarily as a library/translation center; others see it as more of an academy. Recent scholarship suggests it may have been more of a library than a research institution. Present cautiously.

### Slide 39: Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi -- The Father of Algebra
- **Full name:** Abu Abdallah Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi (Arabic: ابو عبد الله محمد بن موسى الخوارزمي)

- **Talking point:** "With almost no formal training, he produced results that astonished the best mathematicians in the world. Hardy called their collaboration 'the one romantic incident in my life.'"

- **Contribution:** First woman to obtain a doctorate in mathematics in modern Europe (Gottingen, 1874). ### Slide 80: David Hilbert and the Foundations Crisis
- **Full name:** David Hilbert
- **Dates:** 1862 - 1943 CE
- **Origin:** Konigsberg, Prussia (modern Kaliningrad, Russia); worked in Gottingen
- **Contribution:** In 1900, posed 23 unsolved problems that shaped 20th-century mathematics. Hilbert's program aimed to put all of mathematics on a provably consistent, complete foundation. Kurt Godel (1906-1978, Austria/USA) proved this was impossible (Incompleteness Theorems, 1931): any sufficiently powerful mathematical system contains true statements that cannot be proved within the system.
- **Visual:** Hilbert's 23 problems listed; Godel's first incompleteness theorem in plain English
- **Talking point:** "Hilbert asked: can we prove that mathematics is free of contradictions? Godel answered: no. And he proved it mathematically."

### Slide 81: Alan Turing -- The Birth of Computer Science
- **Full name:** Alan Mathison Turing
- **Dates:** 1912 - 1954 CE
- **Origin:** London, England; worked at Cambridge, Bletchley Park, Manchester
- **Contribution:** Defined the concept of a "universal computing machine" (the Turing machine, 1936) -- the theoretical foundation of all computers. Proved that some problems are fundamentally unsolvable by any algorithm (the halting problem). Played a central role in breaking the Enigma cipher at Bletchley Park during WWII, building on foundational work by Polish cryptanalysts (Marian Rejewski, Jerzy Rozycki, Henryk Zygalski) who had broken earlier versions of Enigma in the 1930s. [VERIFY: the Polish contribution is well-documented. Rejewski broke the initial Enigma cipher in 1932; the Poles shared their work with British and French intelligence in July 1939. Turing and his team then broke the more complex wartime versions.] Pioneered early ideas about artificial intelligence (the "Turing test").
- **Visual:** Diagram of a Turing machine; photo of the Enigma machine; Turing's portrait; the Bombe machine at Bletchley Park
- **Talking point:** "Polish mathematicians cracked the first version of Enigma. Turing and his team broke the far more complex wartime versions. Every computer, every phone, every AI runs on principles Turing laid down in a 1936 paper."

### Slide 82: John von Neumann
- **Full name:** John von Neumann (born Neumann Janos Lajos)
- **Dates:** 1903 - 1957 CE
- **Origin:** Budapest, Hungary; worked in Germany and the USA (Princeton)
- **Contribution:** Contributions to quantum mechanics, game theory, computer architecture (the "von Neumann architecture" used in virtually all modern computers), cellular automata, nuclear weapons design, and the foundations of mathematics (set theory axiomatization). Possibly the last great polymath.
- **Visual:** The von Neumann architecture diagram (still used today); portrait
- **Talking point:** "The computer you use, the phone in your pocket -- they all follow the architecture he designed in the 1940s"

### Slide 83: John Nash -- Game Theory
- **Full name:** John Forbes Nash Jr.
- **Dates:** 1928 - 2015 CE
- **Origin:** Bluefield, West Virginia, USA; worked at MIT and Princeton
- **Contribution:** Nash equilibrium (1950) -- a foundational concept in game theory where no player can improve their outcome by changing strategy alone. Applications range from economics to evolutionary biology to AI. Nobel Prize in Economics (1994). His life story, including his struggle with schizophrenia and eventual recovery, was portrayed in the film "A Beautiful Mind" (2001).
- **Visual:** A simple game theory payoff matrix illustrating Nash equilibrium; portrait; still from "A Beautiful Mind" (or reference to it)
- **Talking point:** "Every time countries negotiate treaties, companies set prices, or AI agents make decisions -- game theory is at work. And its foundations were laid by a mathematician who nearly lost everything to mental illness."

### Slide 85: Claude Shannon and Information Theory
- **Full name:** Claude Elwood Shannon
- **Dates:** 1916 - 2001 CE
- **Origin:** Petoskey, Michigan, USA; worked at Bell Labs and MIT
- **Contribution:** Founded information theory ("A Mathematical Theory of Communication," 1948). Defined the "bit" as the fundamental unit of information. Showed that all communication can be reduced to binary digits. This is the theoretical foundation for the entire digital age -- the internet, data compression, error correction, cryptography.
- **Visual:** Shannon's communication model diagram; the concept of entropy in information; portrait
- **Talking point:** "Every text message, every video stream, every bit of data on the internet exists because of Shannon's mathematics"

### Slide 86: Edward Lorenz and Chaos Theory
- **Full name:** Edward Norton Lorenz
- **Dates:** 1917 - 2008 CE
- **Origin:** West Hartford, Connecticut, USA; worked at MIT
- **Contribution:** Discovered deterministic chaos (1963) while running a weather simulation. Tiny rounding differences in initial conditions produced wildly different outcomes -- the "butterfly effect." Showed that even perfectly deterministic systems can be practically unpredictable. The Lorenz attractor became one of the iconic images of mathematics.
- **Visual:** The Lorenz attractor (butterfly-shaped strange attractor); a diagram showing sensitive dependence on initial conditions; portrait
- **Talking point:** "He discovered that the flap of a butterfly's wings in Brazil could, in theory, set off a tornado in Texas. Not magic -- mathematics."

### Slide 87: The Four-Color Theorem (1976) -- Computers Enter Mathematics
- **Full name:** Kenneth Appel (1932-2013) and Wolfgang Haken (1928-2022)
- **Dates:** Proof published 1976, at the University of Illinois
- **Key content:** The four-color theorem states that any map can be colored with at most four colors such that no two adjacent regions share a color. Appel and Haken proved this using a computer to check 1,936 [VERIFY: sometimes cited as 1,476 or 1,936] reducible configurations -- the first major theorem to be proved with essential computer assistance. This raised a philosophical question: is a proof that no human can fully verify still a "real" proof?
- **Visual:** A map colored with four colors; a diagram showing why four colors suffice; the 1976 postmark "Four Colors Suffice" used by the UIUC math department [VERIFY: the postmark detail]
- **Talking point:** "For the first time, a computer was essential to proving a mathematical theorem. Some mathematicians refused to accept it. The debate about computer-assisted proofs continues today."

### Slide 88: Benoit Mandelbrot and Fractals
- **Full name:** Benoit B. Mandelbrot
- **Dates:** 1924 - 2010 CE
- **Origin:** Warsaw, Poland (of Lithuanian-Jewish origin); worked in France and at IBM/Yale in the USA
- **Contribution:** Founded fractal geometry. The Mandelbrot set (1980) revealed infinite complexity from simple equations. Showed that many natural phenomena (coastlines, mountains, blood vessels, financial markets) have fractal structure.
- **Visual:** The Mandelbrot set (deep zoom); fractal patterns in nature; portrait
- **Talking point:** "He showed that the messiest, most irregular things in nature -- coastlines, clouds, mountain ranges -- follow hidden mathematical patterns"

### Slide 89: Cryptography -- The Mathematics of Secrecy
- **Key content:** Public-key cryptography, invented in the 1970s, made secure internet communication possible. It is based on number theory -- specifically, the difficulty of factoring large numbers (RSA) or computing discrete logarithms (Diffie-Hellman).
- **Persons:**
  - **Whitfield Diffie** -- Born 1944, USA. Co-invented public-key cryptography (Diffie-Hellman key exchange, 1976).
  - **Martin Hellman** -- Born 1945, USA. Co-invented public-key cryptography with Diffie.
  - **Ron Rivest** -- Born 1947, USA. Co-inventor of RSA algorithm (1977).
  - **Adi Shamir** -- Born 1952, Israel. Co-inventor of RSA algorithm.
  - **Leonard Adleman** -- Born 1945, USA. Co-inventor of RSA algorithm.
- **Visual:** Simplified diagram of how public-key encryption works; the RSA algorithm concept (multiply two large primes -- easy; factor the product -- nearly impossible)
- **Talking point:** "Every time you see the padlock icon in your browser, every time you send a message on Signal or WhatsApp -- you are relying on number theory from the 1970s. The math that Euler and Fermat did for fun now protects your privacy."
- **Interactive moment:** Quick poll -- "Raise your hand if you have used encryption today." (Answer: everyone.) "The math behind that is number theory." (1-2 min)

### Slide 90: Andrew Wiles and Fermat's Last Theorem
- **Full name:** Sir Andrew John Wiles
- **Dates:** Born 1953 CE
- **Origin:** Cambridge, England; worked at Princeton
- **Contribution:** Proved Fermat's Last Theorem in 1995 (after finding and fixing a gap in his 1993 proof). The proof used deep connections between elliptic curves and modular forms (the Taniyama-Shimura-Weil conjecture, now the modularity theorem). This closed a 358-year-old problem.
- **Visual:** The simple statement of Fermat's Last Theorem; Wiles's emotional announcement; the thickness of the proof (over 100 pages)
- **Talking point:** "He worked in secret for seven years. When he announced the proof, he famously choked up with emotion at the end of his lecture."

### Slide 91: 20th Century Overview
- **Key content:** The 20th century transformed mathematics through: foundational crises (Godel), computation theory (Turing), information theory (Shannon), chaos (Lorenz), fractals (Mandelbrot), computer-assisted proof (four-color theorem), cryptography, and game theory (Nash). Mathematics went from a purely abstract discipline to the hidden engine of the digital world.

### Slide 94: Terence Tao -- The Mozart of Math
- **Full name:** Terence Chi-Shen Tao
- **Dates:** Born 1975 CE
- **Origin:** Adelaide, Australia (of Chinese-Hong Kong descent); works at UCLA
- **Contribution:** Fields Medal (2006). Work spans an extraordinary range: harmonic analysis, partial differential equations, analytic number theory, random matrix theory, combinatorics. ### Slide 95: Maryam Mirzakhani -- Breaking Barriers
- **Full name:** Maryam Mirzakhani
- **Dates:** 1977 - 2017 CE
- **Origin:** Tehran, Iran; worked at Stanford University
- **Contribution:** First woman to win the Fields Medal (2014). Worked on the dynamics and geometry of Riemann surfaces and their moduli spaces. Her work connected hyperbolic geometry, complex analysis, and dynamical systems.
- **Visual:** Her characteristic working style (drawing on large sheets of paper on the floor, which her daughter called "painting"); portrait; a Riemann surface visualization
- **Talking point:** "She was the first woman in history to win the Fields Medal. She died of cancer at 40, and Iran put her picture on a postage stamp -- without a headscarf."

### Slide 96: Karen Uhlenbeck -- Geometry and Gauge Theory
- **Full name:** Karen Keskulla Uhlenbeck
- **Dates:** Born 1942 CE
- **Origin:** Cleveland, Ohio, USA; worked at University of Texas at Austin and Princeton
- **Contribution:** First woman to win the Abel Prize (2019), for "pioneering achievements in geometric partial differential equations, gauge theory, and integrable systems." Her work underpins much of modern mathematical physics, including the mathematics behind Yang-Mills gauge theories (which describe fundamental forces in physics). She co-founded the Women and Mathematics program at the Institute for Advanced Study.
- **Visual:** Portrait; a visualization of gauge field geometry; the Abel Prize medal
- **Talking point:** "She won the Abel Prize -- one of math's highest honors -- for work that connects geometry to the fundamental forces of nature. She also spent decades fighting to make mathematics more welcoming to women."
